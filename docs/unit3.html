When you arrive at University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, or even before that, one of the first thing you will learn is to hate Duke. The Carolina-Duke rivalry is not just the greatest rivalry on NCAA basketball field, but also something that has deep impact on both campuses. By reading past news articles and blogs, as well as conversations with several current students, I hope to explore how the rivalry exist on UNC campus and how students perceive, feel or react about it. While the rivalry surely brings students in one school together, it might also induce prejudice, or improper speech and actions against people in another school. In my opinion, the rivalry is a special part of our college experience, and we should enjoy the rivalry and ‚Äúhate‚Äù Duke in a proper way.

What is so special to bring UNC students together is the long-lasting rivalry between University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and Duke University. College rivalry usually forms when schools are located close to each other and/or are competitive in some common fields. Located only about 10 miles apart and both doing well in sports and academics, UNC and Duke have the perfect condition to become rivals and maintain this rivalry. The UNC-Duke rivalry started with two notable families in North Carolina, the Mangums and the Dukes. When a secret ‚Äî unwed Chaney Mangum and married Taylor Duke had given birth to a child ‚Äî was revealed, the relationship of these two families had since then gone bad. And these two families continued to fight for power and dominance in North Carolina. The Mangums were supporters of UNC, while the Dukes used the money from their tobacco industry to support Trinity College, which was later renamed Duke. Slowly, the rivalry between two families transformed into the rivalry between two schools (Frederick, 2019). The modern-day rivalry, on the other hand, exists most prominently in the context of basketball games. However, the rivalry definitely does not limit to basketball, as it can be observed in many aspects of campus life.


The rivalry can be seen in every sport when they play against each other. John Beeson, captain of the club hockey team said ‚ÄúWhether they‚Äôre bad or good, it doesn‚Äôt really matter. No matter what we want to beat them 10‚Äì0.‚Äù Jackson Ozello, a captain on the men‚Äôs club lacrosse team, also agrees that there is an increased focus when facing Duke (Forrest, 2019).


Moreover, the rivalry can be brought up in a different context other than sports. In my computer science class, the professor used a rivalry match as an example to illustrate Java design pattern. My astronomy professor would joke about how he threw away a Duke umbrella belong to his colleague. And across campus, people where shirts writing ‚ÄúBEAT DUKE‚Äù are never hard to find.


They all say that one of the first things you learn at UNC is to ‚Äúhate‚Äù Duke. While some students have quite moderate attitude towards Duke, there are some that go on to the far side of the spectrum. In 1990, student Ian Williams wrote the famous Daily Tar Heel column: Why I Hate Duke. For him, the hatred started from his campus tour to Duke where he met hostile Duke students, and the hatred stayed and grew. He wrote, ‚ÄúI hate every leaf of every tree on that sickening campus. Whether hate Duke a lot or not, rich, proud and overall annoying might be the words many UNC students would think of when describing Duke people. However, this is not true for everyone in Duke. It is correct that Duke students are generally richer, given Duke‚Äôs average annual cost is much higher than UNC ($35735 and $14163 for in-state students), but many Duke students are receiving financial aid from school. For example, I know three people from my high school who went to Duke and they are not especially proud or annoying. I do not imagine them suddenly becomes evil once they enter Duke. In my opinion, despite that ‚Äúhate‚Äù is a very negative word, I do not think it is possible to conclude whether it is proper to hate Duke (or for Duke students to hate UNC), given that everyone defines the word differently. But there are definitely some lines between what is proper or not. If UNC fans and Duke fans start a fight because of a basketball match, it is clearly not proper. Another simple example is seen in a comment section of Daily Tar Heel: a Duke graduate student who was a UNC undergrad wrote that, his/her car got vandalized by Duke students just because the car has Carolina logo on it. Obviously, here the Duke students have crossed the line. And when you hear that US Rep. Brad Miller said ‚ÄúI have said very publicly that if Duke was playing against the Taliban, then I‚Äôd have to pull for the Taliban.‚Äù(Litke, 2012), you know that the line was crossed by a lot, although it was a joke.


The UNC-Duke rivalry is something special, as it magically unifies students within one school. It infiltrates into many aspects of campus life even outside the region of sports, and definitely adds more fun and more topics for students and fans. However, we should also be aware some negative impact it may induce: prejudice and even discrimination are some examples.


As the rivalry divides UNC and Duke into two different sides, it creates an ingroup (‚Äúus‚Äù) and an outgroup (‚Äúthem‚Äù) ‚Äî in this case, UNC and Duke respectively. Social psychology theories (Brown & Hewstone, 2005; Weseley & McEntarffer, 2016) suggest that people tend to like and agree with people within one‚Äôs own group (in-group bias). Some psychologists believe that people inevitably magnify their difference from outgroup members. Combined with in-group bias, this idea indicates that people cannot avoid having stereotypes ‚Äî ideas of what members of another group are like. Stereotypes can be positive or negative, true or false. However, with a general lack of familiarity with outgroup, people are likely to think of outgroup members as similar (out-group homogeneity). Unfortunately, this might subsequently cause prejudice, as someone uncritically apply negative stereotypes to all members of a group, or even discrimination when one acts upon prejudice. 


Whether you hate Duke or not, or how much you hate it, prejudice and discrimination should be avoided. We all know that we should not judge people based on their nationalities, racial identities, gender‚Ä¶ ‚Äì based on the group they are in. And this is similar here (although those students choose to attend Duke). Even if you hate Duke a lot, you should still treat every person with respect first. Blindly labeling everyone at Duke as awful will only hurt nice and innocent people there. After all, there are good and bad people at UNC and at all other schools, and Duke is no exception. College is a place for one to meet different people and accept diversity ‚Äì to get to know ‚Äúout-group members‚Äù, and putting down one‚Äôs prejudice against all Duke people can also be part of the experience. According to contact theory (Cuddy, Fiske, & Glick, 2007; Weseley & McEntarffer, 2016), getting to interact with another out-group member will help reduce prejudice. There are quite some opportunities that UNC students can collaborate with Duke students, and these shall hopefully decrease the hostility against Duke. (Unfortunately for me, I got to collaborate with 2 Duke students at a hackathon ‚Äì and it was not a good experience. Still I know not everyone is like them!)


In my opinion, the rivalry means to compete, thus requires us to be better ‚Äî but it is not limited to being better at a particular sport. It is also an opportunity to have better connection with fellow UNC students. Perhaps, you can make more friends at games or when rushing Franklin, or laugh with your friends some funny jokes and memes about Duke. Besides, the rivalry should not be something to hinder you from being a better person as well. I do not think it is improper to hate Duke as a school if you have good reason. Nonetheless, to prejudice, to hurt individuals just because of the rivalry are not just in themselves wrong, it also makes yourself look bad as well. Meanwhile, it would be better to be open-minded to accept actual nice people from Duke, and do not let the rivalry prevents potential friendships and collaborations. And this not only applies to UNC and Duke students and fans, it can also apply to fans of other colleges, or of different sports clubs as well. In a simple conclusion: enjoy the rivalry!
